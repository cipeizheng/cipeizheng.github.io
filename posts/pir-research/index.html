Private Information Retrieval (PIR) encompasses cryptographic protocols enabling a client to retrieve data from a server-held database without the server learning which data item was accessed. PIR schemes are broadly classified by server model (single-server or multi-server) and security model (computational or information-theoretic). Computational PIR (cPIR), common in single-server settings, relies on cryptographic hardness assumptions like Learning With Errors (LWE) or Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE). Information-Theoretic PIR (IT-PIR), typically multi-server, offers unconditional security but requires non-colluding servers. State-of-the-art (SOTA) advancements as of July 2025 include schemes like Piano and Quarter-PIR for sublinear computation, Pirouette for query efficiency, and Plinko for efficient updates in single-server cPIR. Doubly-Efficient PIR (DEPIR) aims for sublinear server computation and communication, with recent progress in LWE-based constructions. Other advanced concepts include Secret-Key PIR and the application of PIR to Private Information Searching.

1. Introduction to Private Information Retrieval (PIR)#

1.1. Definition and Core Concept#

Private Information Retrieval (PIR) is a sophisticated cryptographic protocol designed to enable a user (client) to retrieve a specific piece of data from a database held by one or more servers without revealing to the server(s) which particular piece of data was accessed , . This is a critical capability in scenarios where data privacy is paramount, such as accessing sensitive financial records, medical information, or proprietary research. The fundamental challenge PIR addresses is how to allow querying a remote database while keeping the query itself private. Traditional methods, like downloading the entire database, are impractical for large datasets due to prohibitive communication costs , . PIR protocols, therefore, aim to achieve this privacy with significantly less communication overhead than the trivial solution of transferring the entire database. The core idea revolves around the user constructing a query that, when processed by the server(s) holding the database, allows the user to extract the desired information, but the server(s) cannot determine which item was retrieved. This is typically achieved through clever cryptographic techniques, often involving the user sending a specially crafted request that “hides” the true intent of the query among other, decoy, requests or by leveraging homomorphic encryption to allow the server to compute on encrypted data without decrypting it. The development of PIR has seen significant advancements, moving from theoretical constructs to more practical implementations, particularly with the advent of single-server computational PIR schemes based on powerful cryptographic primitives like Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) , .

The concept of PIR was first introduced by Chor, Goldreich, Kushilevitz, and Sudan in 1995 , . Their work laid the foundation for two main branches of PIR: information-theoretic PIR (IT-PIR) and computational PIR (cPIR). IT-PIR protocols typically require multiple non-colluding servers, each holding a replica of the database. The user interacts with all servers, and privacy is guaranteed even if the servers have unbounded computational power, as long as they do not collude. The security here is based on the statistical properties of the information shared with each server. On the other hand, cPIR protocols can operate with a single server. Their security relies on computational hardness assumptions, such as the difficulty of factoring large numbers or solving the Learning With Errors (LWE) problem . In cPIR, a computationally bounded server cannot learn any information about the user’s query. While early cPIR schemes were inefficient for practical use, recent advancements, particularly those leveraging FHE, have brought single-server PIR closer to practicality, offering a trade-off where communication and computation costs are significantly reduced compared to IT-PIR, but with security dependent on computational assumptions . The ongoing research in PIR focuses on improving the efficiency of these protocols in terms of communication complexity, computational overhead for both client and server, and exploring new models and applications. Symmetric PIR (SPIR) is a stronger variant where, in addition to query privacy, the client learns nothing about the database other than the retrieved entry, ensuring database privacy as well , .

1.2. Key Properties and Applications#

The primary property of any PIR protocol is query privacy: the server(s) should learn no information about which data item the client is retrieving , . This is the defining characteristic and the main problem PIR aims to solve. Beyond this, other important properties include correctness (the client should correctly retrieve the desired data item), and efficiency, which is typically measured in terms of communication complexity (the total amount of data exchanged between the client and server(s)) and computational complexity (the computational effort required by the client and server(s)) . Early PIR protocols often suffered from high communication or computational costs, making them impractical for large databases. However, significant progress has been made in developing more efficient schemes . For instance, some modern single-server PIR schemes achieve sublinear communication complexity (with respect to the database size), and some even achieve polylogarithmic or constant query sizes, though often at the cost of increased server computation or reliance on preprocessing phases . Some advanced PIR schemes also aim for properties like authenticity, where the client can verify that the retrieved data is indeed from the database and has not been tampered with by a malicious server .

PIR has a wide range of potential applications wherever data privacy is a concern. One prominent area is in privacy-preserving database queries, where users can access public or semi-public databases (e.g., stock market data, genomic databases, patent databases) without revealing their specific interests, which could be commercially sensitive or personally identifiable information , . Another application is in anonymous communication systems, where PIR can be used to retrieve messages or access services without revealing the user’s identity or the specific service being accessed. In the context of outsourced storage, PIR allows clients to retrieve files from a remote storage provider without the provider learning which files are being accessed, thus protecting user access patterns. This is particularly relevant for cloud storage services. Furthermore, PIR techniques are being explored for enhancing privacy in DNS lookups (DNSSEC) , private stream searching, and even in cryptographic protocols like Oblivious RAM (ORAM) and secure multi-party computation (MPC). The ability to privately retrieve information is a fundamental building block for many privacy-enhancing technologies. As PIR protocols become more efficient, their adoption in real-world systems is expected to grow, enabling new services and applications that prioritize user privacy in data retrieval, such as private contact discovery and private auditing .

2. Classification of PIR Protocols#

PIR protocols can be broadly classified based on two main criteria: the server model (single-server vs. multi-server) and the underlying security model (computational vs. information-theoretic). These classifications help in understanding the assumptions made by different protocols and their inherent trade-offs in terms of security, efficiency, and practicality. The choice between these types often depends on the specific application requirements, such as the level of security needed, the availability of infrastructure (e.g., multiple non-colluding servers), and the acceptable performance overhead.

2.1. By Server Model#

The server model refers to the number of servers involved in the PIR protocol and how the database is distributed among them. This is a fundamental distinction that significantly impacts the protocol’s design, security assumptions, and efficiency.

2.1.1. Single-Server PIR#

Single-server PIR (SPIR) protocols, as the name suggests, involve only one server that holds the entire database , . This model is often considered more practical for real-world deployment as it does not require the coordination or trust assumptions associated with multiple servers. However, achieving PIR with a single server is more challenging. In this setting, privacy cannot be information-theoretic; it must rely on computational assumptions. The security of single-server PIR protocols is typically based on the hardness of certain mathematical problems, such as the Learning With Errors (LWE) problem or the difficulty of breaking Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) schemes , . The client sends a query to the single server, which then performs computations on its database and returns a response. The server, if computationally bounded, should not be able to determine which item the client requested. Recent advancements in FHE have led to significant improvements in the efficiency of single-server PIR, making them increasingly viable for practical applications . For example, schemes like XPIR and SealPIR, and more recent ones like OnionPIR and PIRCOR, leverage FHE to achieve sublinear communication complexity, though server-side computation can still be substantial , . The primary advantage of single-server PIR is its simpler deployment model, while the main challenge lies in managing the computational overhead, especially for the server.

The development of single-server PIR has been a major focus of research, driven by the desire for more deployable privacy solutions. Early single-server PIR schemes, like the one proposed by Kushilevitz and Ostrovsky, were based on simpler homomorphic encryption schemes and often had high communication or computation costs , . The introduction of FHE was a game-changer, as it allows the server to perform arbitrary computations on encrypted data. This capability is directly applicable to PIR: the client can encrypt its query (e.g., an index), the server can homomorphically evaluate a function that retrieves the database entry at that index, and return the encrypted result to the client, who then decrypts it. While powerful, FHE-based PIR schemes still face challenges. The computational cost of homomorphic operations can be high, and the ciphertext expansion (the size of encrypted data compared to plaintext) can lead to significant communication overhead, although techniques like batching and ciphertext compression are used to mitigate these issues , . Researchers are continuously working on optimizing FHE schemes and designing PIR protocols that minimize these overheads, exploring trade-offs between communication, computation, and security parameters. Some recent schemes also explore client-side preprocessing to improve online query efficiency or achieve sublinear server computation , .

2.1.2. Multi-Server PIR#

Multi-server PIR protocols involve two or more servers, each holding a copy of the database or a part of it , . The core idea is that the client interacts with all servers, sending each a query. Individually, each query reveals no information about the desired item, but when the responses from all servers are combined, the user can reconstruct the desired data. The key assumption in most multi-server PIR schemes is that the servers are non-colluding, meaning they do not share information about the queries they receive from the client. If a sufficient number of servers (e.g., a majority, or all but one, depending on the specific protocol) do not collude, then the client’s privacy is maintained. Multi-server PIR can achieve information-theoretic security, meaning privacy is guaranteed even against adversaries with unlimited computational power, as long as the non-collusion assumption holds , . This is a significant advantage over single-server PIR, which relies on computational assumptions. Historically, multi-server PIR was the first type of PIR to be developed, with early constructions by Chor et al. demonstrating its feasibility , . These protocols are generally more efficient in terms of computation compared to single-server cPIR, but they incur the overhead of managing multiple server replicas and ensuring the non-collusion assumption.

The efficiency of multi-server PIR protocols is often measured by the number of servers required (k) and the communication complexity. For example, Chor et al. presented a 2-server protocol with O(n1/3) communication complexity and k-server protocols with O(k3n1/(2k-1)) or O(2O(k)n1/k) communication complexity, where n is the database size . There are also protocols that require O(log n) servers with O(log2 n) communication complexity. While these complexities can be sublinear, the constant factors and the practical implications of managing multiple servers (e.g., synchronization, ensuring non-collusion) can be challenging. Some multi-server PIR schemes also consider scenarios where some servers might be unavailable or Byzantine (i.e., they may deviate from the protocol or send incorrect responses). Robust PIR protocols are designed to handle such situations, allowing the client to retrieve the correct data item as long as a sufficient number of honest and responsive servers are available , . The trade-off in multi-server PIR is between the strong security guarantees (information-theoretic privacy) and the practical complexities of deploying and managing multiple, non-colluding servers.

2.2. By Security Model#

The security model defines the nature of the privacy guarantee offered by a PIR protocol. This classification is crucial as it dictates the types of adversaries the protocol can withstand and the underlying cryptographic assumptions.

2.2.1. Computational PIR (cPIR)#

Computational PIR (cPIR) protocols ensure privacy against adversaries that are computationally bounded, typically meaning they cannot solve certain hard mathematical problems in polynomial time , . These protocols rely on cryptographic assumptions, such as the hardness of factoring large integers, the discrete logarithm problem, or, more commonly in modern schemes, the Learning With Errors (LWE) problem and the security of Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) , . In a cPIR scheme, even if a server (or an eavesdropper) sees the client’s query and the server’s response, they cannot determine which item was retrieved, assuming their computational power is limited. Single-server PIR protocols are inherently computational, as information-theoretic privacy is impossible with a single, curious server , . The primary advantage of cPIR is that it can be implemented with a single server, simplifying deployment. However, the reliance on computational assumptions means that if these assumptions are broken (e.g., by advances in algorithms or quantum computing), the privacy guarantee could be compromised. The efficiency of cPIR schemes has improved significantly with the development of FHE, allowing for protocols with sublinear communication complexity, though server computation remains a concern .

The evolution of cPIR has been closely tied to advancements in public-key cryptography and, more recently, homomorphic encryption. Early cPIR schemes often involved the client sending a query that was an encryption of the desired index, and the server would perform computations (e.g., exponentiation in a group) to generate a response that, when decrypted by the client, revealed the desired data. These schemes often had communication complexity proportional to the database size or high computational costs. The breakthrough of FHE, starting with Gentry’s work, opened up new possibilities for cPIR. FHE allows the server to perform arbitrary computations on ciphertexts. For PIR, this means the client can send an FHE encryption of its query (e.g., a vector with a ‘1’ at the desired index and ‘0’s elsewhere), and the server can homomorphically compute the inner product of this encrypted query vector with the database (also represented as a vector), resulting in an FHE ciphertext of the desired data item. This approach allows for very flexible and powerful PIR constructions. Modern cPIR schemes like XPIR, SealPIR, OnionPIR, and PIRCOR are based on lattice-based FHE schemes (often Ring-LWE based) and employ various optimizations like batching, modulus switching, and query compression to improve performance , . Despite these advances, the computational overhead of FHE operations remains a significant factor in the practicality of these schemes, especially for very large databases.

2.2.2. Information-Theoretic PIR (IT-PIR)#

Information-Theoretic PIR (IT-PIR) protocols provide a stronger notion of privacy: the server(s) learn absolutely nothing about the client’s query, even if they have unbounded computational power , . This level of security is achieved without relying on unproven computational assumptions. However, IT-PIR protocols typically require multiple non-colluding servers, each holding a copy of the database , . The client’s query is distributed among these servers in such a way that no individual server (or no small coalition of servers, depending on the protocol’s threshold) can gain any information about the index of the item being retrieved. The privacy guarantee stems from the statistical properties of the information held by each server. For example, in a simple 2-server IT-PIR scheme, the client might send a random string to one server and a related string (such that their XOR reveals the desired index) to the other. Each server responds based on its query and its copy of the database. The client then combines these responses to obtain the desired data. Individually, each server’s view (its query and its database) reveals no information about the client’s intent.

The primary advantage of IT-PIR is its strong security guarantee, which is not dependent on the hardness of computational problems. This makes it resilient to future algorithmic breakthroughs or the advent of quantum computers (from a computational perspective, though other quantum effects might be relevant). However, IT-PIR comes with its own set of challenges. The most significant is the requirement for multiple, non-colluding servers. Ensuring non-collusion can be difficult in practice, especially if servers are operated by different entities with potential incentives to collude. Communication complexity is another factor; while IT-PIR schemes can achieve sublinear communication, the constants involved and the number of servers required can be higher than in some cPIR schemes. Classic IT-PIR constructions include those by Chor et al. , which established the field. These protocols often involve the client secret-sharing its query among the servers, and the servers then computing and returning secret-shares of the answer, which the client can then reconstruct. Robust IT-PIR schemes also exist, designed to tolerate server failures or Byzantine behavior, ensuring that the client can still retrieve the correct data as long as a sufficient number of servers are honest and responsive , . The trade-off is between the robust, information-theoretic security and the practical overhead of managing multiple server replicas and the non-collusion assumption.

2.3. Other Emerging PIR Types#

Beyond the traditional classifications, several specialized and emerging types of PIR have been developed to address specific requirements or improve upon existing models. These often combine elements of single/multi-server and computational/information-theoretic approaches.

2.3.1. Symmetric Key PIR (SK-PIR)#

Symmetric Key PIR (SK-PIR) refers to PIR protocols where the client and server share a secret key. This model is distinct from standard PIR where the server typically holds only a public database. SK-PIR can offer advantages in certain scenarios, such as when the client is expected to perform multiple queries and a long-term secret relationship with the server is acceptable. Some SK-PIR schemes aim for very efficient server computation by leveraging the shared secret. For instance, recent research has explored SK-DEPIR (Secret-Key Doubly Efficient PIR) and its relationship with black-box cryptographic constructions , . Another line of work focuses on constructing SK-PIR from random linear codes, aiming for practical efficiency , . The security in SK-PIR relies on the secrecy of the shared key and the cryptographic primitives used. The “symmetric” in SK-PIR refers to the use of a symmetric key, not to be confused with Symmetric PIR (SPIR) which refers to protocols that also protect database privacy. Recent research has focused on constructing SK-PIR from assumptions like Learning Parity with Noise (LPN) or Learning Subspace with Noise (LSN), aiming for practical efficiency and strong security .

2.3.2. Functional PIR (FPIR)#

Functional PIR (FPIR), also known as Private Information Retrieval by Keywords (PIR-KW) or Private Queries on Public Data (PQPD), extends the basic PIR functionality. Instead of retrieving an item by its index, FPIR allows the client to retrieve data based on a function or a keyword. For example, a client might want to retrieve all records in a database that match a certain keyword, or perform a more complex computation on the data and retrieve the result, all without revealing the function or keyword used. This is a more powerful primitive than standard PIR, as it supports richer query types. Constructing efficient and secure FPIR schemes is generally more challenging than standard PIR. Techniques often involve combining PIR with other cryptographic primitives like searchable encryption, oblivious RAM (ORAM), or fully homomorphic encryption (FHE). The security definition for FPIR needs to carefully consider what information is leaked to the server, such as the access pattern to the database or the size of the result set. The goal is to minimize such leakage while maintaining practical efficiency. FPIR has applications in private database querying, private data analysis, and privacy-preserving machine learning, where users need to retrieve specific information or perform computations on public datasets without revealing their private interests or queries.

2.3.3. Attribute-Based PIR (AB-PIR)#

Attribute-Based PIR (AB-PIR) is a more advanced concept that combines PIR with Attribute-Based Encryption (ABE). In AB-PIR, a client can retrieve data from a server if and only if their attributes satisfy a certain access policy defined over the data. The server learns neither the queried data item nor the client’s attributes or access policy, beyond what is inherently leaked by the fact that access was granted. This provides a fine-grained access control mechanism integrated with private retrieval. For example, a medical database might be structured such that only doctors with a specific specialization can retrieve certain patient records. An AB-PIR scheme would allow a doctor to privately query for such a record, and the server would only return the record if the doctor’s credentials (attributes) match the policy associated with the record, without learning which record was accessed or the details of the doctor’s credentials. Constructing practical AB-PIR schemes is a complex task, as it requires integrating the complexities of both PIR and ABE. The efficiency of such schemes, particularly in terms of communication and computation for both client and server, is a major research challenge. AB-PIR has potential applications in secure and private data sharing systems where access control needs to be enforced without compromising the privacy of the data or the users’ queries and attributes.

2.3.4. Doubly-Efficient PIR (DEPIR)#

Doubly-Efficient PIR (DEPIR) is an advanced PIR concept that aims to achieve sublinear server computation time with respect to the database size, in addition to sublinear communication complexity , . This is a significant improvement over traditional PIR schemes where the server typically needs to perform computations that are at least linear in the size of the database for each query. The “doubly-efficient” aspect refers to efficiency in both computation (for the server) and communication. A breakthrough in this area was the construction by Lin, Mook, and Wichs (STOC’23), which presented the first DEPIR scheme based on the Ring Learning With Errors (Ring-LWE) assumption , . This construction remains one of the few known DEPIR schemes. A key challenge in DEPIR is to realize such schemes from alternative, potentially weaker, cryptographic foundations, such as plain LWE, without relying on the ring structure. Recent research, as of July 2025, has made progress in this direction. For instance, a new LWE-based DEPIR construction in the Common Reference String (CRS) model has been proposed, inspired by SimplePIR and leveraging matrix-vector multiplication preprocessing techniques , . While this construction achieves server computation of O(N/log N), which is only “barely” sublinear, it represents an important step as it was previously unknown if such modest sublinear efficiency could be achieved from unstructured, plain LWE . Further research is focused on improving the sublinearity factor and exploring other cryptographic assumptions for DEPIR.

3. State-of-the-Art Single-Server Computational PIR#

Single-server computational PIR (cPIR) has seen remarkable advancements, largely driven by progress in Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) and lattice-based cryptography. These schemes aim to provide practical privacy for database queries with the convenience of a single server, albeit with security reliant on computational assumptions. The state-of-the-art (SOTA) in this area focuses on optimizing communication complexity, reducing server and client computation, and improving overall efficiency for real-world deployment.

3.1. Lattice-Based PIR Schemes#

Lattice-based cryptography, particularly schemes based on the Learning With Errors (LWE) problem and its variants like Ring-LWE, forms the foundation of many modern FHE constructions and, consequently, many SOTA single-server cPIR protocols. The security of these schemes is based on the presumed hardness of certain problems on lattices, which are believed to be resistant to quantum attacks, making them attractive for post-quantum cryptography.

3.1.1. Schemes based on Learning With Errors (LWE)#

The Learning With Errors (LWE) problem is a fundamental problem in lattice cryptography that involves distinguishing noisy inner products from random. Many FHE schemes are built upon LWE or its ring variant (Ring-LWE). PIR schemes based directly on LWE often involve the client sending an LWE-based encryption of its query (e.g., an index or a selection vector), and the server performs homomorphic operations to retrieve and encrypt the desired data item. While Ring-LWE based schemes often offer better efficiency due to algebraic structure, plain LWE based constructions are also explored for their potential simplicity or different security properties. For instance, the SimplePIR scheme, highlighted for its conceptual simplicity and efficiency, is based on LWE and focuses on optimizing the core matrix-vector multiplication that underlies many FHE-based PIR schemes , . Recent research has also explored Doubly-Efficient PIR (DEPIR) from LWE, aiming for schemes where the server’s online computation is sublinear in the database size, often by leveraging preprocessing . While achieving full DEPIR from plain LWE with optimal parameters is still an open problem, constructions like the one presented by Lee (ePrint Report, 2025) show progress in building LWE-based DEPIR, even if they are “barely doubly-efficient” with server computation like O(N/log N) , . These schemes often involve techniques like the matrix-vector multiplication preprocessing of Williams (SODA’07) to achieve sublinear online server work.

The challenge with LWE-based PIR (and FHE in general) is managing the noise growth during homomorphic operations. Each operation increases the noise in the ciphertext, and if it becomes too large, decryption fails. Techniques like bootstrapping (which homomorphically evaluates the decryption function to “refresh” a ciphertext) or modulus switching (reducing the ciphertext modulus to reduce noise at the cost of fewer supported operations) are used to manage noise. The choice of LWE parameters (dimension, modulus, error distribution) directly impacts both security and efficiency. Larger parameters generally provide higher security but also lead to larger ciphertexts and slower operations. PIR schemes built directly on LWE often involve encoding the database and query in a way that allows the server to perform an inner product or a similar operation homomorphically. For example, the client might send an LWE encryption of a unit vector (with ‘1’ at the desired index and ‘0’s elsewhere), and the server computes the dot product of this encrypted vector with the database (treated as a vector of LWE ciphertexts or plaintexts). The efficiency of this operation is critical. Some SOTA schemes focus on optimizing this core operation, for instance, by using techniques like the Number Theoretic Transform (NTT) for fast polynomial multiplication, which is a key component in many lattice-based cryptographic operations .

3.1.2. Ring-LWE based Schemes#

Ring-LWE is a variant of LWE that operates over polynomial rings, offering significant efficiency advantages due to its algebraic structure, which allows for the use of techniques like the Number Theoretic Transform (NTT) for fast polynomial multiplication. Many of the most efficient FHE schemes, and consequently many SOTA single-server cPIR protocols, are based on Ring-LWE. Examples include XPIR , SealPIR (which builds upon and optimizes XPIR), and OnionPIR . These schemes typically represent the database and queries as elements in a polynomial ring, and the retrieval operation is performed via homomorphic multiplication and addition of these ring elements. The use of Ring-LWE allows for more compact ciphertexts and faster homomorphic operations compared to plain LWE in many cases, leading to better overall performance in terms of both communication and computation. For instance, XPIR was an early practical demonstration of FHE-based PIR using a Ring-LWE based FHE scheme, showing computational efficiency but also highlighting challenges with communication cost . Subsequent work, like SealPIR and OnionPIR, focused on improving these aspects, for example, by optimizing parameters, using ciphertext compression, or structuring the database in a way that minimizes the number of homomorphic operations required.

A key technique in Ring-LWE based PIR is batching, which allows a single ciphertext to encrypt a vector of plaintext values. This is achieved by using the Chinese Remainder Theorem (CRT) over polynomial rings. Batching enables the server to perform Single Instruction, Multiple Data (SIMD) operations homomorphically, processing multiple database elements in parallel with a single homomorphic operation. This can lead to a significant speedup in server computation. For example, if the database is viewed as a matrix, and each row is encoded into a single batched ciphertext, the PIR query (also a batched ciphertext representing a selection vector) can homomorphically select and sum the appropriate rows. The PIRCOR and PIRCOR* schemes, for instance, utilize the Brakerski-Fan-Vercauteren (BFV) homomorphic encryption scheme (which is Ring-LWE based) and employ NTT for accelerating encryption operations, including ciphertext-plaintext and ciphertext-ciphertext multiplications . These schemes also explore optimizations like query size reduction through techniques like Replicated Sums of Matrices (RSM) in PIRCOR* and offline preprocessing to shift computational load from the online phase . The ongoing research in this area focuses on further optimizing these building blocks, improving parameter selection, and developing new protocols that offer better trade-offs between security, communication, and computation.

3.2. Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) based PIR#

Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) allows arbitrary computations to be performed directly on encrypted data without needing to decrypt it first. This property is incredibly powerful for PIR: a client can encrypt its query (e.g., the index of the desired item), send it to the server, the server homomorphically evaluates a function that retrieves the item from the (plaintext) database, and returns the encrypted result to the client. The client then decrypts this result to obtain the desired data. Most SOTA single-server cPIR schemes are built using FHE, particularly FHE schemes based on lattice problems like LWE and Ring-LWE , . The development of FHE has been a major catalyst for advancements in cPIR, enabling constructions that were previously not feasible. Early FHE schemes were quite impractical, but significant progress has been made in improving their efficiency, leading to more viable FHE-based PIR protocols. When PIR is instantiated with FHE, the protocol can be non-interactive, requiring only a single query-answer exchange, and it can achieve asymptotically optimal communication and computation complexity , .

3.2.1. Performance Considerations and Optimizations#

While FHE enables powerful PIR constructions, its performance remains a critical consideration. The main challenges are the computational overhead of homomorphic operations and the communication cost associated with large FHE ciphertexts. Server-side computation can be intensive, as homomorphic multiplications and additions are significantly slower than their plaintext counterparts. Communication costs arise because FHE ciphertexts are much larger than the plaintext data they encrypt (ciphertext expansion). Several optimization techniques are employed to address these issues:

  1. Batching: As mentioned earlier, batching allows multiple plaintext values to be packed into a single FHE ciphertext. The server can then perform SIMD-style operations, processing many database elements in parallel with a single homomorphic instruction. This significantly amortizes the computational cost per database element , .
  2. Ciphertext Compression/Encoding: Techniques are used to reduce the size of ciphertexts or queries. For example, clients might only send a subset of ciphertext components if others can be reconstructed or are not needed for the specific PIR protocol step . Some schemes also use more efficient encoding of the database to minimize the number of homomorphic operations required.
  3. Database Layout and Homomorphic Evaluation Strategy: Optimizing how the database is structured (e.g., as a hypercube or a tree) and how the homomorphic selection function is evaluated can significantly impact performance. Hypercube-based structures, as used in XPIR, SealPIR, and OnionPIR, allow for efficient homomorphic selection by reducing the problem to a series of smaller selections across dimensions . Tree-based PIR, such as SHECS-PIR, offers an alternative structure that can achieve optimal logarithmic communication complexity with a smaller constant factor than some hypercube approaches, requiring exactly log₂n ciphertexts for a query .
  4. Query Packing and Unpacking: Multiple query bits or database elements can be packed into a single FHE ciphertext to amortize costs. Different algorithms for query unpacking and homomorphic operations (e.g., plaintext-ciphertext multiplication vs. ciphertext-ciphertext multiplication) can significantly impact performance .
  5. Parallelization: Exploiting parallel processing capabilities on the server to speed up homomorphic computations.
  6. Noise Management: Efficiently managing the noise growth in FHE ciphertexts to ensure correct decryption without excessive bootstrapping (if the FHE scheme supports it and it’s beneficial).

A 2025 study highlighted that a less-explored FHE-based PIR design could achieve a 90% reduction in communication cost and an 8x decrease in computational overhead compared to other designs, challenging common misconceptions about their relative efficiencies , . This underscores the importance of careful protocol design and analysis.

3.3. Preprocessing PIR (PPIR)#

Preprocessing PIR (PPIR) refers to PIR schemes that involve an initial, potentially expensive, offline phase (the preprocessing phase) to generate some auxiliary information (a “hint” or “state”) that can be used to make subsequent online PIR queries more efficient , . This model is particularly attractive when the client plans to make multiple queries to a relatively static database, as the cost of preprocessing can be amortized over many queries. The goal is to achieve sublinear online communication and computation for both client and server . Preprocessing can occur on the server side, the client side, or both. For example, the server might preprocess the database to create a more efficient data structure for PIR, or the client might perform some offline computations to generate a local state that speeds up query generation . Information-theoretic PPIR with client preprocessing has been shown to achieve online client/server computation and bandwidth sublinear in the database size, with the offline phase cost being a one-time expense .

3.3.1. Schemes with Tight Space-Time Trade-offs#

A significant area of research in PPIR is achieving optimal or near-optimal trade-offs between the client’s storage for the preprocessed hint, the online communication complexity, and the server’s online computation. For single-server PPIR, a 2024/2025 work presented schemes with nearly optimal client space and bandwidth trade-offs . One such scheme, adapting a 2-server PIR scheme to a 1-server preprocessing model, achieves (o(1)) online bandwidth and (O(n/t)) offline bandwidth, with client space of (t \cdot n^{o(1)}) and server computation of (n^{1+o(1)}), after a one-time preprocessing phase where the client streams the database with (O(n)) bandwidth and server computation . Another scheme aims for sublinear server computation, achieving (O(n^{2/3})) server computation in the online phase, with online bandwidth of (O(n^{1/3})) and client space of (O(n^{2/3})), after a similar one-time preprocessing . These results demonstrate the potential of preprocessing to significantly improve online efficiency, though achieving sublinear server computation often comes at the cost of increased client storage or communication. An open problem mentioned is whether a scheme can achieve client space, bandwidth, and server computation all bounded by (O(n^{1/2})) . A EUROCRYPT 2025 paper discusses constructing 2-server preprocessing PIR with polylogarithmic bandwidth using only one-way functions, consuming $\widetilde{O}\lambda(N^{\frac12 + \eps})$ client space and $N^{1+\eps}$ server space . In the single-server setting, the same work achieves polylogarithmic online bandwidth and $\widetilde{O}\lambda(N^{\frac12 + \eps})$ offline bandwidth with similar client and server space .

3.3.2. Schemes based on Decisional Composite Residuosity (DCR)#

The Decisional Composite Residuosity (DCR) assumption has proven to be a fertile ground for constructing efficient cryptographic primitives, including PIR schemes, particularly those involving preprocessing. As highlighted in the EUROCRYPT 2025 proceedings, an “Optimal Preprocessing PIR from DCR” was presented , . This scheme allows for a client and server, after an initial setup phase involving a broadcast of (o(n)) bits, to engage in multiple PIR queries with optimal communication: the client sends (\log(n)) bits, and the server replies with 1 bit (or a small constant number of bits if blocks are retrieved) . The DCR assumption, which posits that it is computationally hard to distinguish random elements from (N)-th powers in (\mathbb{Z}_{N^2}^*) (where (N) is a product of two large primes), provides a strong foundation for building secure and efficient protocols. In the context of PIR, DCR-based constructions often utilize homomorphic properties. For example, the Paillier cryptosystem, which is secure under the DCR assumption, allows for the addition of plaintexts via multiplication of ciphertexts. This property can be leveraged in PIR schemes where the server’s response is a homomorphically computed function of the database and the query. The efficiency gains in the online phase of such DCR-based PPIR schemes stem from the ability to perform complex computations on encrypted data (the query) that translate to simple operations on the plaintext database, or from clever ways of structuring the precomputed data to facilitate fast retrieval. The security relies on the hardness of the DCR problem, ensuring that the server cannot learn any information about the client’s query index from the interaction.

3.4. Advanced SOTA Single-Server Schemes (as of July 2025)#

The field of single-server PIR continues to evolve rapidly, with new schemes and optimizations emerging to improve efficiency, security, and functionality. As of July 2025, several advanced schemes represent the state-of-the-art. These predominantly fall under the computational PIR (cPIR) model, where security relies on cryptographic assumptions. The EUROCRYPT 2025 conference proceedings serve as a key indicator of the state-of-the-art, showcasing several novel constructions that push the boundaries of PIR efficiency and functionality , . These include schemes leveraging invertible pseudorandom functions (iPRFs) for efficient updates, algebraic homomorphic encryption for doubly-efficient PIR, and specialized LWE-based constructions for query efficiency. The common goal is to move PIR closer to practical viability for a wider range of applications by reducing the inherent costs associated with privacy-preserving data retrieval from a single, untrusted server.

3.4.1. Piano and Quarter-PIR: Sublinear Computation and Communication#

A significant breakthrough in practical PIR is the development of schemes that achieve sublinear server computation and communication, moving beyond the traditional linear computation barrier where the server must process the entire database for each query. The PhD thesis “Private Information Retrieval and Searching with Sublinear Costs” by Mingxun Zhou, anticipated in July 2025, proposes two such novel PIR schemes: Piano and Quarter-PIR , . These schemes are designed to provide near real-time responses even for very large databases, containing billions of entries, while maintaining reasonable communication and storage requirements , . The core idea behind these constructions is to leverage preprocessing techniques, potentially combined with advanced cryptographic tools, to allow the server to perform work proportional to a sublinear function of the database size (e.g., (O(\sqrt{n})) or (O(n^{2/3}))) during the online query phase. This dramatically improves performance compared to schemes requiring full database scans. The thesis highlights that these contributions aim to bridge the gap between theoretical PIR research and practical application, opening new avenues for privacy-preserving systems in large-scale settings . The PIANO scheme, in particular, is notable for achieving sublinear server computation from the minimal assumption of one-way functions (OWF) . For a 100GB database, PIANO achieved a 73ms response time, compared to an estimated 11 seconds or more for SimplePIR, representing over a 150x speedup . The practical utility of Piano and Quarter-PIR is further demonstrated through their application to private information searching, specifically in the development of Pacmann, a private approximate nearest neighbor search algorithm capable of handling hundreds of millions of records , .

3.4.2. Pirouette: Query Efficient Single-Server PIR#

Pirouette is a query-efficient single-server PIR protocol detailed in a May 2025 Cryptology ePrint Archive paper , . It focuses on minimizing the size of the query sent from the client to the server, a critical aspect for practicality, especially as database sizes grow. Pirouette achieves compact queries by having the client send a fresh Learning With Errors (LWE) ciphertext, but with a crucial optimization: the client sends only the ((n+1))-th component of the LWE ciphertext along with a pseudorandom generator (PRG) seed; the server can then use this seed to pseudorandomly generate the first (n) components . This significantly reduces the upload cost. The database in Pirouette is structured as a hypercube using polynomial rings, similar to some prior FHE-based PIR schemes like Respire . A novel contribution of Pirouette is a procedure that enables the server to homomorphically extract the encrypted query indices for each dimension of the hypercube database from this single LWE ciphertext. These extracted indices are then used to homomorphically select the desired database record. For a database with (N=2^{25}) records, Pirouette reports a query size of 336 bytes, matching the query size achieved by T-Respire (Respire with transciphering) but without the associated 16.2x increase in running time that T-Respire incurs compared to the original Respire . Pirouette adopts a sophisticated, cross-scheme approach, strategically leveraging a suite of Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) schemes (BPV, GSW, FHEW, TFHE) and state-of-the-art optimizations to minimize both computational overhead and communication complexity . It utilizes LWE, RLWE, RGSW, and notably RLWE’ ciphertexts, with RLWE’ aimed at minimizing noise growth during homomorphic operations .

3.4.3. Plinko: Single-Server PIR with Efficient Updates#

A common challenge in many PIR schemes, especially those involving preprocessing or specific data structures, is handling updates to the database efficiently. The Plinko protocol, presented at EUROCRYPT 2025, directly addresses this issue by proposing a single-server PIR scheme with efficient updates , . Plinko is a significant advancement in single-server PIR with client pre-processing, achieving optimal trade-offs between client storage (r) and total query time (t) for all parameters, specifically t = $\tilde{O}(n/r)$. This matches the known lower bounds of r · t = Ω(n) up to logarithmic factors, a notable improvement over previous schemes that could only achieve this optimality when client storage r was around $\tilde{O}(\sqrt{n})$ . Beyond this core efficiency gain, Plinko is also distinguished as the first updateable PIR scheme where a database entry can be modified in worst-case $\tilde{O}(1)$ time . This feature is crucial for dynamic databases. The core technical tool enabling this efficiency is the concept of an invertible pseudorandom function (iPRF). An iPRF generalizes standard PRFs by equipping them with an efficient inversion algorithm. The Plinko paper presents a construction of an iPRF from one-way functions where forward evaluation runs in $\tilde{O}(1)$ time, and inversion runs in time linear in the inverse set (output) size , . This iPRF construction is claimed to be the first that remains efficient and secure for arbitrary domain and range sizes, including small domains and ranges . In the context of single-server PIR, these iPRFs are used to construct a “hint set representation” where finding a hint containing a specific database entry (x) can be done in $\tilde{O}(1)$ time, crucial for enabling efficient updates.

3.4.4. Other Notable Recent Single-Server PIR Constructions#

Beyond the specifically named protocols, the EUROCRYPT 2025 proceedings highlight other significant advancements in single-server PIR. These include works focusing on the theoretical underpinnings and potential limitations of certain approaches. For instance, one paper explores “Algebraic Homomorphic Encryption and its Applications to Doubly-Efficient PIR,” suggesting new pathways to achieve PIR schemes where both client and server computations are highly efficient . Another, titled “Black Box Crypto is Useless for Doubly Efficient PIR,” likely provides important lower bounds or impossibility results, indicating that generic use of cryptographic primitives may not suffice to achieve the desired efficiency goals in DEPIR, potentially necessitating custom-built algebraic constructions or stronger assumptions , . Furthermore, the topic of “Single-Server Client Preprocessing PIR with Tight Space-Time Trade-off” was also addressed, indicating ongoing efforts to optimize the balance between the client’s storage for preprocessed hints and the online query/response efficiency . Other notable schemes include CB-cPIR, a code-based cPIR scheme proposed as an alternative to lattice-based schemes, claiming to be concretely cheaper in both communication and computational costs, deriving security from the hardness of decoding a random linear code , . SimplePIR remains a practical benchmark for single-server PIR due to its highly optimized linear server computation , . List-Decodable PIR (LD-PIR) allows for recovery of the desired item even if some server responses are corrupted, with variants achieving No(1) communication complexity . These diverse approaches highlight the active research in making single-server PIR more practical and efficient.

4. State-of-the-Art Multi-Server PIR#

Multi-server PIR traditionally offers information-theoretic security, relying on the assumption that servers do not fully collude. Recent advancements focus on improving efficiency, reducing the number of servers, and exploring new models like client preprocessing.

4.1. Information-Theoretic Multi-Server PIR#

Information-Theoretic PIR (IT-PIR) in the multi-server setting provides the strongest form of privacy, secure against computationally unbounded adversaries, as long as a threshold number of servers do not collude.

4.1.1. Classic Constructions and Evolutions#

The foundational work by Chor, Goldreich, Kushilevitz, and Sudan introduced IT-PIR with multiple servers , . These early schemes often had high communication complexity. Subsequent research focused on reducing this complexity. For example, protocols achieving O(n^(1/c)) communication complexity for c servers were developed. A significant evolution is the introduction of client preprocessing in the IT-PIR model. This allows the client to perform an offline phase with the servers, storing a local state (hint) that enables more efficient online queries. Corrigan-Gibbs and Kogan (Eurocrypt 2020) initiated significant work in this area . The goal is to achieve sublinear online client/server computation and bandwidth, amortizing the cost of an expensive offline phase over many queries. This model is particularly interesting as it can leverage symmetric key primitives or public key assumptions like DDH or LWE, but recent work also explores unconditional IT-PIR with preprocessing . Evolutions of these classic constructions have focused on improving communication complexity, reducing the number of servers required, and enhancing robustness against server failures or Byzantine behavior , .

4.1.2. SOTA IT-PIR with Improved Efficiency#

Recent advancements in IT-PIR focus on enhancing efficiency in various models:

  • IT-PIR with Client Preprocessing (Unconditional): A notable December 2024 / November 2021 (conference date/publication history) paper presents an unconditional IT-PIR protocol for 2t servers (where t is between 2 and log₂n/2) with a threshold of 1 (i.e., privacy holds if at least one server is honest). This protocol achieves O~(n^(1-1/t)) client and server online computation, matching the computation costs of other works based on cryptographic assumptions. The client storage is O~(n^(0.5+1/2t)) and online communication is O~(n^(1/2)). A key advantage is its concretely efficient online computation, dominated by XOR operations rather than slower cryptographic operations. This work introduces a new primitive called “privately multi-puncturable random set” (PMPRS) as a building block .
  • IT-PIR in the Shuffle Model: Another line of work explores IT-PIR in the shuffle model, where queries from multiple clients are anonymized by a shuffle mechanism. A May 2025 paper , presents the first single-server IT-PIR protocol in this model with sublinear per-client communication and information-theoretic security. After one-time server-side preprocessing, the protocol requires only sublinear per-client computation. Concretely, for any γ > 0, the protocol has O(n^γ) communication and computation costs per client, with 1/poly(n) statistical security, assuming poly(n) clients access a size-n database simultaneously. This contrasts with doubly efficient PIR in the standard model, which is inherently limited to computational security .
  • 2-Server PPIR with Sublinear Computation: A 2024/2025 ePrint paper , (Theorem 1.5) describes a 2-server IT-PIR scheme with client preprocessing that achieves sublinear computation. The preprocessing phase requires O(n^(2/3)) bandwidth, O(n^(2/3)) client computation, and O(n) server computation. The online query phase achieves O(n^(1/3)) online bandwidth, O(n^(1/2)) client computation, O(n^(2/3)) server computation, and O(n^(2/3)) client space , .
  • Robust IT-PIR: A May 2024 conference paper detailed a robust PIR protocol combining cryptographic and information-theoretic techniques, achieving up to 9.1x lower latency and 39.2x less total communication than previous SOTA robust PIR protocols for a 4GB database, capable of withstanding two malicious servers .
  • Symmetric PIR (SPIR) with Sublinear Online Time: A recent paper (Cryptology ePrint Archive 2025/1313) introduces the first two-server SPIR protocols that achieve both sublinear online time and statistical security, along with enhancements for sublinear amortized time .

4.2. Computational Multi-Server PIR#

While multi-server PIR is often associated with information-theoretic security, computational PIR can also be deployed in a multi-server setting, potentially offering different trade-offs in terms of efficiency, the number of servers required, or resilience to server collusion.

4.2.1. BatchPIR and Related Approaches#

Batch Private Information Retrieval (BatchPIR) allows a client to retrieve multiple data items from a database in a single query or a small number of queries, with the goal of amortizing the server’s computational cost per retrieved item. This concept is applicable to both single-server and multi-server PIR settings , . In the multi-server context, BatchPIR can leverage the inherent parallelism and distributed nature of the servers to further improve efficiency. The first BatchPIR scheme, named Amortized PIR, was proposed by Ishai et al. and utilized batch codes . It achieved server computation of O(N^(3/2)log b) for retrieving a batch of size b. The state-of-the-art BatchPIR scheme mentioned in early 2025 is due to Angel et al. . More recent advancements include “Vectorized BatchPIR,” which focuses on achieving low communication and computation, particularly for databases with small entry sizes. This scheme uses vectorized homomorphic encryption and a method to merge ciphertexts encrypting independent entries, reportedly achieving a 7.5x to 98.5x reduction in response overhead compared to the previous state-of-the-art BatchPIR . For example, to download a batch of 256 entries from a database of one million entries (each 256 bytes), Vectorized BatchPIR achieved an amortized communication overhead of 19.2x over an insecure baseline, with an amortized computation cost of about 123 milliseconds . A 2025 work, BitBatSPIR, focuses on batch symmetric PIR for bit entries, constructing it from Private Set Intersection (PSI) protocols, leveraging efficient PSI protocols for asymmetric set sizes and claiming stronger security and better performance for large databases , .

5. Doubly-Efficient PIR (DEPIR) - A Deeper Look#

Doubly-Efficient PIR (DEPIR) aims to achieve sublinear computational complexity for both the client and the server during the online query phase, a significant improvement over traditional PIR where server computation is often linear in the database size.

5.1. Concept and Motivation for DEPIR#

The primary motivation for Doubly-Efficient Private Information Retrieval (DEPIR) is to make PIR practical for very large databases by reducing the substantial computational burden on the server, which is a major bottleneck in many PIR schemes , . “Doubly efficient” signifies that both the client’s and the server’s online workloads are significantly less than scanning the entire database. This is often achieved by introducing a preprocessing phase where the server (and sometimes the client) performs some work upfront to enable fast online queries , . For example, the server might preprocess the database to create a “hint” or an encoded version of the data, or the client might perform offline computations to generate a local state . The challenge lies in designing such preprocessing and online protocols that are both efficient and secure, ideally based on standard cryptographic assumptions. The concept also extends to scenarios where the client might have a small, persistent local storage to hold a preprocessed state, allowing for very fast queries over time . The goal is to achieve online complexities that are, for example, polylogarithmic or a small fractional power (e.g., (n^\epsilon)) of the database size (n). The introduction of DEPIR opens up possibilities for deploying PIR in scenarios where database sizes are massive and server resources are a concern, potentially enabling new applications that were previously infeasible due to performance constraints.

5.2. LWE-Based DEPIR Constructions#

Lattice-based cryptography, particularly schemes relying on the Learning With Errors (LWE) problem and its variants like Ring-LWE, has been instrumental in developing DEPIR constructions.

5.2.1. Recent Advances and Open Problems (e.g., plain LWE vs. Ring-LWE)#

A landmark achievement in DEPIR was the construction by Lin, Mook, and Wichs (LMW) presented at STOC’23, which provided the first DEPIR scheme based on the Ring Learning With Errors (Ring-LWE) assumption , . This construction remains a foundational piece of work in the field. However, a significant open problem has been to realize DEPIR from alternative, and potentially more standard or “plain” LWE assumptions, without relying on the algebraic structure of rings. As of July 2025, progress is being made in this direction. A recent ePrint report from July 19, 2025, details a new LWE-based DEPIR construction set in the Common Reference String (CRS) model , . This scheme draws inspiration from SimplePIR and utilizes the matrix-vector multiplication preprocessing technique developed by Williams (SODA’07). While this construction achieves a server computation of O(N/log N), which is described as “barely doubly-efficient,” it is a crucial step forward because it was previously unknown if even such modest sublinear efficiency could be achieved from unstructured, plain LWE , . This work also shows that, by applying a compiler from Lin, Mook, and Wichs (Eurocrypt’25), an sk-DEPIR (a keyed variant of DEPIR) can be obtained based on LWE in the standard model , . The distinction between Ring-LWE and plain LWE is significant because plain LWE is often considered a more conservative assumption. Therefore, constructions based on plain LWE are highly desirable. The ongoing research aims to bridge this gap further, seeking DEPIR schemes from plain LWE that offer more substantial sublinearity factors and potentially better concrete efficiency. Recent advances also include the PIANO scheme, which achieves sublinear server computation from one-way functions, a weaker assumption than LWE , and the development of invertible pseudorandom functions (iPRFs) from one-way functions, which can be used to construct efficient hint set representations for DEPIR .

5.3. Limitations and Challenges in DEPIR Realization#

Despite the promising advancements, the realization of practical and widely deployable DEPIR schemes faces several limitations and challenges. One primary challenge is the actual degree of sublinearity achieved. While O(N/log N) server computation is technically sublinear, the improvement over linear schemes might be marginal for practically sized databases unless the constant factors are very favorable . Achieving more aggressive sublinearity, such as O(√N) or even polylog(N), for server computation remains a significant open problem, especially under standard cryptographic assumptions. Another challenge is the cost and complexity of the offline preprocessing phase. While this phase is amortized over many queries, it must still be computationally feasible and should not result in an excessively large preprocessed database representation. The reliance on specific cryptographic assumptions, like Ring-LWE or the need for a Common Reference String (CRS) in some plain LWE constructions, can also be a limitation if these assumptions are later weakened or if the CRS model is deemed impractical for certain applications . Furthermore, the communication complexity, while sublinear, needs to be concretely efficient. Balancing the trade-offs between server computation, communication, client computation, and the strength of underlying assumptions is a delicate act. Finally, the security proofs for DEPIR schemes can be complex, and ensuring robust security against various adversarial models is paramount. Addressing these limitations is the focus of ongoing research in the DEPIR community. A significant theoretical limitation, highlighted by an ePrint report from March 2025, is that unconditionally secure “split-key” Secret-Key DEPIR (SK-DEPIR) cannot exist if the size of the secret key component used simultaneously for database preprocessing and client query generation is bounded by a fixed polynomial and yet the scheme aims to support an arbitrary polynomial number of PIR queries . This implies a fundamental trade-off between key size, security level, and the longevity or query capacity of the DEPIR system.

6. Other Advanced PIR Concepts and Trends (as of July 2025)#

Beyond the traditional classifications, the field of Private Information Retrieval continues to evolve with new concepts and trends aimed at improving efficiency, security, and applicability. These advancements often build upon existing PIR techniques or introduce novel cryptographic tools to address specific challenges. As of mid-2025, several such trends are notable, reflecting the ongoing research efforts to make PIR more practical and versatile for a wider range of applications.

6.1. Secret-Key PIR from Random Linear Codes#

Secret-Key PIR (SK-PIR) is a variant of PIR where the client and server share a secret key, which is used to facilitate the private retrieval process. This is in contrast to the more common public-key or information-theoretic PIR settings. The introduction of a shared secret key can potentially lead to more efficient protocols, as the server can use the key to preprocess the database or the client can use it to generate queries in a way that reduces the overall communication or computation burden. One line of research in this area explores the construction of SK-PIR schemes based on random linear codes. The security of such schemes often relies on the hardness of decoding random linear codes, which is a well-studied problem in coding theory. The use of coding theory in PIR is not new; many IT-PIR schemes leverage error-correcting codes. However, SK-PIR from random linear codes specifically focuses on the secret-key setting and aims to achieve efficiency by exploiting the properties of these codes. The shared key might be used to generate a pseudorandom code or to permute the database entries in a key-dependent manner, making it difficult for an adversary without the key to determine the queried item even if they observe the queries and responses. The development of such schemes often involves careful analysis of the trade-offs between the key size, the security level, and the efficiency parameters of the PIR protocol. While potentially offering performance benefits, SK-PIR schemes inherently require a key distribution mechanism and are limited to scenarios where a pre-shared secret exists between the client and the server, which may not be suitable for all applications. Recent research has focused on constructing SK-PIR from assumptions like Learning Parity with Noise (LPN) or Learning Subspace with Noise (LSN), aiming for practical efficiency and strong security .

6.2. Distributional PIR#

Distributional PIR is a concept that considers scenarios where the database entries are not arbitrary but are drawn from some known probability distribution, or where the client’s queries are not uniformly distributed across the database. The goal of distributional PIR is to design protocols that are more efficient (in terms of communication or computation) when such distributional information is available and can be leveraged. For example, if certain database items are queried more frequently than others, a distributional PIR scheme might try to optimize the retrieval of these popular items. Similarly, if the database itself has a known structure (e.g., many entries are identical or very similar), this information might be used to reduce the cost of PIR. The security definitions for distributional PIR need to be carefully formulated to ensure that privacy is still maintained even when the distributions are known. This might involve requiring that the server’s view of the query is indistinguishable for any two indices that have the same probability under the query distribution, or that the server learns no additional information beyond what is implied by the known distributions. Research in distributional PIR explores how to incorporate this statistical information into the protocol design to achieve better performance compared to generic PIR schemes that make no assumptions about the data or query distributions. This area is less explored than standard PIR but offers potential for efficiency gains in specific application contexts where such distributional knowledge is available or can be accurately modeled.

6.3. Lower Bounds and Impossibility Results#

The study of lower bounds and impossibility results is crucial in PIR research, as it helps to understand the fundamental limitations of what can be achieved in terms of efficiency and security. These results provide theoretical limits on the performance of PIR protocols, guiding researchers away from unproductive avenues and highlighting the inherent trade-offs. For example, it is well-known that information-theoretic PIR with a single server is impossible without downloading the entire database. This is a fundamental impossibility result. Other lower bounds often relate to the communication complexity of PIR protocols. For instance, for multi-server IT-PIR, there are lower bounds on the communication complexity as a function of the number of servers and the database size. In the context of computational PIR, lower bounds might relate the computational complexity to the strength of cryptographic assumptions. For example, a paper titled “Black Box Crypto is Useless for Doubly Efficient PIR*” (ePrint March 2025) provides important lower bounds, indicating that generic use of cryptographic primitives may not suffice to achieve the desired efficiency goals in DEPIR, potentially necessitating custom-built algebraic constructions or stronger assumptions , . This work shows that unconditionally secure “split-key” Secret-Key DEPIR (SK-DEPIR) cannot exist if the size of the secret key component used simultaneously for database preprocessing and client query generation is bounded by a fixed polynomial and yet the scheme aims to support an arbitrary polynomial number of PIR queries . Such results are vital for understanding the landscape of PIR and for setting realistic expectations for future protocol designs.

6.4. Private Information Searching (PIS) using PIR#

Private Information Searching (PIS), also known as searchable encryption or private keyword search, extends the concept of PIR to allow clients to search for data in a database based on keywords or more complex predicates, rather than just retrieving an item by its index. While PIR focuses on hiding which item is being accessed, PIS aims to hide what is being searched for. This is a significantly harder problem, as the server needs to perform a search operation without learning the search query or the result. PIR can be used as a building block for PIS. For example, if the database is an inverted index mapping keywords to lists of document identifiers, a PIR protocol could be used to privately retrieve the list of identifiers associated with a particular keyword. However, this often requires careful design to ensure that the overall PIS system remains efficient and secure. The NIST Computer Security Resource Center’s “Special Topics on Privacy and Public Auditability — Event 3” held in July 2021 featured discussions on “private information retrieval (PIR); searchable encryption; fully homomorphic encryption (FHE)” , . Searchable encryption is a closely related concept to PIS, and FHE is a powerful tool that can enable PIS by allowing the server to perform search operations on encrypted queries and data. The challenge in PIS is to achieve sublinear search time (relative to the database size) while maintaining strong privacy guarantees for both the query and the results. Recent advancements in efficient PIR, such as the Piano and Quarter-PIR schemes, have been applied to private information searching, for example, in the development of Pacmann, a private approximate nearest neighbor search algorithm capable of handling hundreds of millions of records , .

7. Conclusion and Future Directions in PIR Research#

Private Information Retrieval has evolved significantly since its inception, transitioning from theoretical curiosities to a vibrant field with increasingly practical protocols. The state-of-the-art in July 2025 showcases a diverse landscape of PIR schemes, each with its own set of trade-offs. Single-server computational PIR has seen remarkable progress, driven by advancements in lattice-based cryptography and Fully Homomorphic Encryption. Schemes like Piano, Quarter-PIR, Pirouette, and Plinko are pushing the boundaries of efficiency, offering sublinear computation, query optimization, and dynamic updates. Multi-server PIR continues to offer strong information-theoretic security, with ongoing research focused on improving communication complexity, robustness, and exploring models like client preprocessing and the shuffle model. Doubly-Efficient PIR (DEPIR) remains a key research goal, with recent breakthroughs in LWE-based constructions bringing us closer to practical sublinear server computation.

Looking ahead, several future directions are poised to shape PIR research. Enhancing practical efficiency across all types of PIR will remain a primary focus, aiming to reduce concrete overheads in communication, computation, and storage to make PIR viable for even larger and more demanding applications. This includes further optimizing FHE-based schemes, developing more efficient DEPIR constructions from standard assumptions, and improving the preprocessing phases in PPIR. Strengthening security models is another important avenue, encompassing research into authenticated PIR, verifiable PIR (where the client can verify the correctness of the response), and PIR with enhanced privacy guarantees against stronger adversarial models. Exploring new PIR variants and functionalities, such as Attribute-Based PIR, Functional PIR for richer query types, and PIR for specialized data structures or computational tasks (like private machine learning inference), will broaden the applicability of PIR. Furthermore, post-quantum security will continue to be a critical consideration, with a focus on developing and standardizing PIR schemes based on cryptographic assumptions believed to be resilient to quantum attacks. Finally, bridging the gap between theory and practice through robust implementations, standardized benchmarks, and real-world deployment case studies will be crucial for the widespread adoption of PIR technologies. As privacy concerns continue to grow in our increasingly data-driven world, PIR is set to play an increasingly vital role in enabling secure and private data access.
